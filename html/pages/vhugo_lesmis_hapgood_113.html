2.3.7.headCHAPTER VII—COSETTE SIDE BY SIDE WITH THE STRANGER IN THE DARK
2.3.7.1Cosette, as we have said, was not frightened.
2.3.7.2The man accosted her. He spoke in a voice that was grave and almost bass.
2.3.7.3“My child, what you are carrying is very heavy for you.”
2.3.7.4Cosette raised her head and replied:—
2.3.7.5“Yes, sir.”
2.3.7.6“Give it to me,” said the man; “I will carry it for you.”
2.3.7.7Cosette let go of the bucket-handle. The man walked along beside her.
2.3.7.8“It really is very heavy,” he muttered between his teeth. Then he added:—
2.3.7.9“How old are you, little one?”
2.3.7.10“Eight, sir.”
2.3.7.11“And have you come from far like this?”
2.3.7.12“From the spring in the forest.”
2.3.7.13“Are you going far?”
2.3.7.14“A good quarter of an hour’s walk from here.”
2.3.7.15The man said nothing for a moment; then he remarked abruptly:—
2.3.7.16“So you have no mother.”
2.3.7.17“I don’t know,” answered the child.
2.3.7.18Before the man had time to speak again, she added:—
2.3.7.19“I don’t think so. Other people have mothers. I have none.”
2.3.7.20And after a silence she went on:—
2.3.7.21“I think that I never had any.”
2.3.7.22The man halted; he set the bucket on the ground, bent down and placed both hands on the child’s shoulders, making an effort to look at her and to see her face in the dark.
2.3.7.23Cosette’s thin and sickly face was vaguely outlined by the livid light in the sky.
2.3.7.24“What is your name?” said the man.
2.3.7.25“Cosette.”
2.3.7.26The man seemed to have received an electric shock. He looked at her once more; then he removed his hands from Cosette’s shoulders, seized the bucket, and set out again.
2.3.7.27After a moment he inquired:—
2.3.7.28“Where do you live, little one?”
2.3.7.29“At Montfermeil, if you know where that is.”
2.3.7.30“That is where we are going?”
2.3.7.31“Yes, sir.”
2.3.7.32He paused; then began again:—
2.3.7.33“Who sent you at such an hour to get water in the forest?”
2.3.7.34“It was Madame Thénardier.”
2.3.7.35The man resumed, in a voice which he strove to render indifferent, but in which there was, nevertheless, a singular tremor:—
2.3.7.36“What does your Madame Thénardier do?”
2.3.7.37“She is my mistress,” said the child. “She keeps the inn.”
2.3.7.38“The inn?” said the man. “Well, I am going to lodge there to-night. Show me the way.”
2.3.7.39“We are on the way there,” said the child.
2.3.7.40The man walked tolerably fast. Cosette followed him without difficulty. She no longer felt any fatigue. From time to time she raised her eyes towards the man, with a sort of tranquillity and an indescribable confidence. She had never been taught to turn to Providence and to pray; nevertheless, she felt within her something which resembled hope and joy, and which mounted towards heaven.
2.3.7.41Several minutes elapsed. The man resumed:—
2.3.7.42“Is there no servant in Madame Thénardier’s house?”
2.3.7.43“No, sir.”
2.3.7.44“Are you alone there?”
2.3.7.45“Yes, sir.”
2.3.7.46Another pause ensued. Cosette lifted up her voice:—
2.3.7.47“That is to say, there are two little girls.”
2.3.7.48“What little girls?”
2.3.7.49“Ponine and Zelma.”
2.3.7.50This was the way the child simplified the romantic names so dear to the female Thénardier.
2.3.7.51“Who are Ponine and Zelma?”
2.3.7.52“They are Madame Thénardier’s young ladies; her daughters, as you would say.”
2.3.7.53“And what do those girls do?”
2.3.7.54“Oh!” said the child, “they have beautiful dolls; things with gold in them, all full of affairs. They play; they amuse themselves.”
2.3.7.55“All day long?”
2.3.7.56“Yes, sir.”
2.3.7.57“And you?”
2.3.7.58“I? I work.”
2.3.7.59“All day long?”
2.3.7.60The child raised her great eyes, in which hung a tear, which was not visible because of the darkness, and replied gently:—
2.3.7.61“Yes, sir.”
2.3.7.62After an interval of silence she went on:—
2.3.7.63“Sometimes, when I have finished my work and they let me, I amuse myself, too.”
2.3.7.64“How do you amuse yourself?”
2.3.7.65“In the best way I can. They let me alone; but I have not many playthings. Ponine and Zelma will not let me play with their dolls. I have only a little lead sword, no longer than that.”
2.3.7.66The child held up her tiny finger.
2.3.7.67“And it will not cut?”
2.3.7.68“Yes, sir,” said the child; “it cuts salad and the heads of flies.”
2.3.7.69They reached the village. Cosette guided the stranger through the streets. They passed the bakeshop, but Cosette did not think of the bread which she had been ordered to fetch. The man had ceased to ply her with questions, and now preserved a gloomy silence.
2.3.7.70When they had left the church behind them, the man, on perceiving all the open-air booths, asked Cosette:—
2.3.7.71“So there is a fair going on here?”
2.3.7.72“No, sir; it is Christmas.”
2.3.7.73As they approached the tavern, Cosette timidly touched his arm:—
2.3.7.74“Monsieur?”
2.3.7.75“What, my child?”
2.3.7.76“We are quite near the house.”
2.3.7.77“Well?”
2.3.7.78“Will you let me take my bucket now?”
2.3.7.79“Why?”
2.3.7.80“If Madame sees that some one has carried it for me, she will beat me.”
2.3.7.81The man handed her the bucket. An instant later they were at the tavern door.
