2.4.2.headCHAPTER II—A NEST FOR OWL AND A WARBLER
2.4.2.1It was in front of this Gorbeau house that Jean Valjean halted. Like wild birds, he had chosen this desert place to construct his nest.
2.4.2.2He fumbled in his waistcoat pocket, drew out a sort of a pass-key, opened the door, entered, closed it again carefully, and ascended the staircase, still carrying Cosette.
2.4.2.3At the top of the stairs he drew from his pocket another key, with which he opened another door. The chamber which he entered, and which he closed again instantly, was a kind of moderately spacious attic, furnished with a mattress laid on the floor, a table, and several chairs; a stove in which a fire was burning, and whose embers were visible, stood in one corner. A lantern on the boulevard cast a vague light into this poor room. At the extreme end there was a dressing-room with a folding bed; Jean Valjean carried the child to this bed and laid her down there without waking her.
2.4.2.4He struck a match and lighted a candle. All this was prepared beforehand on the table, and, as he had done on the previous evening, he began to scrutinize Cosette’s face with a gaze full of ecstasy, in which the expression of kindness and tenderness almost amounted to aberration. The little girl, with that tranquil confidence which belongs only to extreme strength and extreme weakness, had fallen asleep without knowing with whom she was, and continued to sleep without knowing where she was.
2.4.2.5Jean Valjean bent down and kissed that child’s hand.
2.4.2.6Nine months before he had kissed the hand of the mother, who had also just fallen asleep.
2.4.2.7The same sad, piercing, religious sentiment filled his heart.
2.4.2.8He knelt beside Cosette’s bed.
2.4.2.9lt was broad daylight, and the child still slept. A wan ray of the December sun penetrated the window of the attic and lay upon the ceiling in long threads of light and shade. All at once a heavily laden carrier’s cart, which was passing along the boulevard, shook the frail bed, like a clap of thunder, and made it quiver from top to bottom.
2.4.2.10“Yes, madame!” cried Cosette, waking with a start, “here I am! here I am!”
2.4.2.11And she sprang out of bed, her eyes still half shut with the heaviness of sleep, extending her arms towards the corner of the wall.
2.4.2.12“Ah! mon Dieu, my broom!” said she.
2.4.2.13She opened her eyes wide now, and beheld the smiling countenance of Jean Valjean.
2.4.2.14“Ah! so it is true!” said the child. “Good morning, Monsieur.”
2.4.2.15Children accept joy and happiness instantly and familiarly, being themselves by nature joy and happiness.
2.4.2.16Cosette caught sight of Catherine at the foot of her bed, and took possession of her, and, as she played, she put a hundred questions to Jean Valjean. Where was she? Was Paris very large? Was Madame Thénardier very far away? Was she to go back? etc., etc. All at once she exclaimed, “How pretty it is here!”
2.4.2.17It was a frightful hole, but she felt free.
2.4.2.18“Must I sweep?” she resumed at last.
2.4.2.19“Play!” said Jean Valjean.
2.4.2.20The day passed thus. Cosette, without troubling herself to understand anything, was inexpressibly happy with that doll and that kind man.
